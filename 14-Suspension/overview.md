# Suspension — Coilovers + Full Refresh

## Status: Research Complete, Not Yet Purchased

## My Build Philosophy

At 170,000 miles, every rubber bushing, shock, and joint in my suspension is degraded. Rather than just replacing worn parts with OEM equivalents, I'm treating this as an opportunity to do a complete suspension overhaul — coilovers for adjustable ride height and damping, fresh polyurethane bushings throughout, upgraded sway bars, and proper alignment correction for a lowered car.

This fits my build's daily/sport duality perfectly: coilovers with adjustable damping can be softened for daily commuting and stiffened for spirited driving, just like my valved exhaust switches between quiet and loud.

## Component-by-Component: Options, Research, and Buy Links

### 1. Coilovers — Going with coilovers, no question

Coilovers replace the front strut assemblies (shock + spring) and rear shocks + springs with a single adjustable unit per corner. Ride height adjustment and (on most) damping adjustment.

**What I was looking for:**
- Monotube design (better heat dissipation than twin-tube)
- 30+ clicks of damping adjustment (more = finer tuning)
- Pillow ball front camber plates (allows camber adjustment without separate bolts)
- Rebuildable/revalveable (extends lifetime, allows future tuning)
- Appropriate spring rates for street/sport use (6-8 kg/mm front, 4-6 kg/mm rear)

### 8. Roll Center Adjusters — Must-Have if Dropping 1.5"+

When you lower a MacPherson strut car, the lower ball joint angle changes, moving the roll center below the ground plane. This actually makes the car handle **worse** — more body roll, more weight transfer, and bump steer. Roll center adjusters (extended lower ball joints) restore proper geometry.

## Installation Notes

### Difficulty: Advanced — 12-20 hours DIY, or $800-1,500 professional labor

This is a full suspension teardown and rebuild. I'm planning to do it myself, so here's what I'm looking at:

- **Jack stands are mandatory.** I'll be under the car for extended periods.
- **Spring compressor is NOT needed** — coilovers come pre-assembled. I'm removing complete strut assemblies, not compressing springs.
- **Bushing removal requires a press** for most bushings. I'll need a shop press or bushing removal tool set.
- **Rear trailing arm bushing removal** is notoriously difficult. A lot of DIYers have a shop press out the old bushings and press in new ones. I might go that route.
- **I'm doing ALL bushing work, ball joints, tie rods, and coilovers in one session.** The car is already apart — doing this in phases would be insane.
- **Label everything.** I'll photograph every connection before disassembly.
- **Anti-seize on all bolts** during reassembly.
- **Torque all fasteners to spec** — no guessing.
- **Alignment immediately after.** I won't drive more than necessary before getting aligned.

### My plan of attack:
1. Buy all parts before starting
2. Remove front and rear suspension one side at a time
3. Press out old bushings, press in new poly bushings
4. Install coilovers, new ball joints, tie rods, sway bars
5. Install rear camber arms
6. Set approximate ride height (adjust later)
7. Drive carefully to alignment shop
8. Get performance alignment
9. Fine-tune ride height and damping over the next week
10. Re-torque all fasteners after 100 miles
