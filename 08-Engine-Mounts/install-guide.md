# Engine Mounts Install Guide — Hybrid Racing 70A

## Status: Purchased, install pending

The full kit is in the box — passenger, driver (transmission), rear, and front torque/lower mount. $657.15 spent. Last piece of the drivetrain-feel puzzle, going in alongside the already-installed Exedy Stage 1 clutch, Exedy lightweight flywheel, Acuity short shifter, and Acuity trans-side cable bushings.

---

## Reality Check Before I Start

**Intermediate-level, 3-4 hour job** if nothing goes sideways. Every mount is 2-4 bolts and a swap — the difficulty is what's around the mounts:

- Engine has to be supported the entire time a mount is out. Drop it and I'm buying an oil pan at best, a pickup tube at worst.
- Harnesses, coolant hoses, and the brake line route near the passenger and rear mounts. Pinch one and I've added a new problem.
- Access is tight up top. I'll be removing the K&N intake tube (and probably the air box) to see the passenger mount (→ [01-Cold-Air-Intake/](../01-Cold-Air-Intake/)).

**Where people get hurt:** rushing the engine support. Jack under the oil pan without a wide wood block dents the pan (stamped steel on the K20Z3). Engine support bar over the shock towers is safer and leaves both hands free.

**Why 70A and not stiffer:** reliability-first decision. 88A+ polyurethane mounts on the FG2 are known to crack the stamped OEM mount *brackets* (not the mounts — the brackets they bolt into) over time. The 8th gen passenger mount bracket is a particular weak point. 70A gives me the vast majority of the drivetrain-tightening benefit without transmitting enough load into the brackets to tear them. Stiffer is not better past this point on this chassis.

## Prerequisites / Cross-references

### Accept before install

- **NVH will go up, permanently.** Idle buzz through the shift knob, pedals, and steering wheel will be noticeably stronger. Cold-start vibration is the most obvious change for the first 30 seconds each morning. At driving RPM it mostly disappears. This is the documented 70A tradeoff — livable, but real.
- **Clunk on throttle on/off transitions is normal on polyurethane mounts.** It's the small amount of drivetrain rotation the stiffer bushings allow snapping to the loaded position. Fades over break-in. Loud metallic banging is different — stop and inspect.

## Tools Required

**Critical:**
- **Engine support bar** (Harbor Freight item 61238 or similar, ~$80) OR a floor jack with a wide piece of 2x6 or 2x8 hardwood to spread the load across the oil pan. Support bar is strongly preferred.
- 14mm, 17mm, 19mm sockets (6-point, not 12-point — these bolts will be tight)
- Matching wrenches in the same sizes (for the nut-on-the-back-side fasteners)
- Breaker bar, 18" minimum — rear mount bolts are torqued to 47 ft-lb from factory and haven't been out since 2009 in my car
- **Torque wrench, 10-80 ft-lb range** (3/8" drive preferred for the mount bolts)
- Needle-nose pliers — for the small harness clips around the mount brackets
- Pry bar, 12" — for nudging the engine laterally to line up new mount bolt holes
- Floor jack + 4-ton jack stands (for the car itself, separate from engine support)
- Penetrating oil (PB Blaster) — mount bolts are weather-exposed for 17 years

**Also useful:**
- Impact gun (makes initial bolt removal much faster, but do final install with torque wrench)
- Magnetic parts tray — I will lose a bolt down into the belly pan otherwise
- Droplight / headlamp
- Sharpie to mark bolt locations and engine position before anything comes out

## Step-by-Step

### Phase A: Lift and Access (20-30 min)

1. Let the car sit cold. Park on level ground. Chock the rear wheels.
2. Disconnect the battery (-) terminal. Mounts are not electrical, but the passenger mount area is right next to the alternator B+ cable and I don't need that cable arcing against a wrench.
3. Lift the front of the car, set on 4-ton jack stands on the subframe pinch welds. Both sides. Never work under a jack alone.
4. Drop the belly pan (plastic undertray) — several 10mm bolts and plastic pushpins. Set aside.
5. Spray penetrant on every mount bolt I can see, top and bottom. Let sit while I set up the engine support.

### Phase B: Engine Support (15-20 min)

6. Position the engine support bar across the shock towers, over the engine. Feet resting on the flat shock tower tops (use a towel or thin rubber under each foot to protect the paint).
7. Attach the support chain/hook to the engine lift point on the cylinder head (the cast eyelet on the back of the head, driver side, is factory-intended for this).
8. Take up slack in the chain. I want the engine supported but **not lifted** yet. The suspension should still be carrying the weight through the subframe.
9. Alternative if no support bar: position a floor jack under the oil pan with a 2x8 board across the pan to spread load over the widest area possible. **Do not jack directly on the oil pan metal — it will dent.** The pan is stamped steel and the pickup tube is right above it.

### Phase C: Passenger-Side (Upper) Mount FIRST (45-60 min)

Most accessible mount, and with the support bar at tension the engine can't move while I work.

10. Remove the K&N intake tube between air box and throttle body (3-4 hose clamps, MAP vacuum line). See [01-Cold-Air-Intake/](../01-Cold-Air-Intake/) for hose routing on reassembly.
11. If the air box blocks mount bolts (it will, on the forward corner), pop it out. 2 bolts + MAF connector.
12. **Take a phone photo** of mount orientation, bracket orientation, and bolt positions before anything comes out.
13. Remove 2-3 bolts holding the mount bracket to the **engine** (17mm).
14. Remove 3 bolts holding the mount to the **chassis** (14mm).
15. Lift the mount + bracket out as one assembly.
16. **Lift the engine slightly** with the support bar — just 1/4" to 1/2" — to clear the new mount's bolt holes. No more than necessary; I don't want to stress the other mounts or axles.
17. Position the new HR mount. **Orientation matters** — look for the alignment pin or the arrow cast into the housing. Mounting backward loads the urethane at the wrong angle and causes premature failure.
18. Install all bolts hand-tight first, then torque per the HR sheet. **Typical spec is 40-54 ft-lb depending on the bolt.** Factory reference is 47 ft-lb on the side mount through-bolts ([docs/torque-specs.md](../docs/torque-specs.md)). Blue Loctite on chassis-side bolt threads.
19. Release support bar tension and let the engine settle. Should sit square, no sag. Re-tension for the next mount.

### Phase D: Rear Mount (Engine Block to Subframe) (30-45 min)

This one takes the most torque reaction load in 1st and 2nd gear — biggest contributor to wheel hop on a soft mount.

20. Under the car, locate the rear mount. 3 bolts — 2 into the subframe cradle, 1 through-bolt to the engine bracket.
21. **Support bar under tension before pulling the through-bolt.** Don't let the engine shift rearward.
22. Remove the 2 subframe bolts first. Through-bolt last.
23. Pull the old mount down and out.
24. Install the new HR rear mount — asymmetric, find the alignment feature and install in the factory-intended direction.
25. Torque through-bolt and subframe bolts per HR spec (47 ft-lb factory reference, but **HR sheet is the authority on HR parts**). Blue Loctite.

### Phase E: Driver-Side (Transmission) Mount (45-60 min)

26. Top of the transmission, bolted to the chassis frame rail. Access from above.
27. **Watch the harness routing.** Trans harness is zip-tied to the mount bracket in a couple places on the FG2 — cut and save those positions, I'll replace on reassembly.
28. Remove bolts holding bracket to trans case (17mm).
29. Remove bolts holding mount to frame rail (typically 3x 14mm).
30. Lift mount + bracket out as one assembly.
31. Install new HR trans mount. Mind orientation, torque to spec, blue Loctite.
32. Re-route harness with new zip ties. **Do not pinch the harness between bracket and frame rail** — chafes through over time and throws random electrical codes.

### Phase F: Front Lower / Torque Mount (30-45 min)

The "dogbone" mount runs from the front of the engine down to the subframe. Controls engine rotation under throttle.

33. Locate the torque mount: small rectangular dogbone, 2 bolts.
34. **Watch for exhaust proximity.** The downpipe and stock manifold pass very close — if the exhaust is still hot, wait.
35. Remove the 2 bolts. Tilt the mount to clear the subframe.
36. Install the new HR torque mount. **Most orientation-critical of the four** — the urethane bushings at each end are loaded differently under forward vs reverse torque. Factory-intended direction only.
37. Torque to HR spec (typically 40-47 ft-lb). Blue Loctite.

### Phase G: Final Checks and Reassembly (20-30 min)

38. **Torque every single bolt** per the HR instruction sheet. Mount bolts take shock loads — under-torqued bolts walk loose.
39. **Gently test engine lift** with the support bar after each mount and once after all four. Engine should settle square, no twist or sag. If sagging, back out and check mount orientation.
40. Release support bar fully. Engine rides on the new mounts.
41. Visual sweep:
    - No pinched harness (especially driver-side trans mount area)
    - No hose touching exhaust or moving parts
    - Intake tube fully seated, hose clamps tight
    - No dropped hardware sitting on the subframe
42. Reinstall air box, intake tube, belly pan.
43. Lower car off jack stands. Reconnect battery (-).

## Post-Install Break-In

Polyurethane mounts settle over the first several heat cycles. Urethane compresses slightly under the engine's steady weight, and the torque on the bolts relaxes as the mount cures into its final position.

- **First 500 miles:** drive normally. No repeated hard launches. Let the mounts settle.
- **At ~500 miles:** re-torque every mount bolt. I will find 1-2 that have backed off slightly. Non-optional.
- **At ~1000 miles:** final re-torque pass. Stable after this.

Skipping the re-torque is how people end up with a mount bolt backing out at 10k miles and a half-loose mount they don't know about.

## First Drive Checklist

- **Idle vibration** — expected, moderate. Buzz through the shift knob and pedals is normal. Violent shaking is not — stop and inspect (wrong orientation, loose bolt, mount not seated).
- **Shift feel** — tighter immediately. Less engine rock during gear changes. With the short shifter and bushings, shifts should feel surgical.
- **Clunk on throttle on/off transitions** — **normal on 70A.** Fades over break-in. Loud metallic banging is NOT normal.
- **Wheel hop under hard launches** — dramatically reduced. Biggest functional win of the upgrade.
- **Cold start** — noticeably more vibration the first 15-30 seconds. Smooths as engine warms.
- **Axles** — listen for new clicking or binding. Stiffer mounts can reveal a tired axle at 170k by letting more load through.

---

## Common Mistakes

1. **Mount orientation wrong.** Urethane mounts are asymmetric and directional. Backward loads the insert at the wrong angle and it fails premature. Alignment pin or arrow on every mount before bolting.
2. **Forgetting the 500-mile re-torque.** Bolts loosen as urethane seats. Re-torque or accept eventual walk-out.
3. **Pinching the trans harness** between driver-side mount bracket and frame rail. Chafes through and throws random electrical codes.
4. **Engine on the bare oil pan without a wood block.** Dents the pan. Worst case dents the pickup tube bracket inside and starves the engine of oil under hard cornering. Wide 2x8 minimum, support bar preferred.
5. **Skipping the phone photo before disassembly.** I promise I'll remember bracket orientation. I won't.
6. **Over-torquing.** HR spec is 40-54 ft-lb depending on bolt. Torquing to 80+ "to make sure" strips the stamped engine-side brackets. Brackets are the weak point, not the bolts.

## Expected NVH Changes — Honest Version

Gaining:

- Significantly less wheel hop on hard launches
- Tighter shift feel (compounds with the short shifter and bushings)
- Sharper throttle response (less drivetrain slop eating inputs)
- Engine and drivetrain move as one unit under load

Giving up:

- **More cabin buzz at idle** through the shift knob, steering wheel, pedals. Noticeable at every light.
- **More cold-start vibration** the first 15-30 seconds.
- **Slight highway drone** around 2500-3000 RPM in 6th. Drive around it by staying off that exact RPM.
- **Clunk on throttle transitions** during break-in.

Net: tradeoff is worth it. Every FG2 owner running 70A in the research threads says the same thing — livable, and the drivetrain-feel gain is real.
