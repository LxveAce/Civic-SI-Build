# Clutch Hydraulics Upgrade — 2009 Honda Civic SI (FG2 / K20Z3)

**Vehicle:** 2009 Civic SI Coupe (FG2), K20Z3, ~170,000 miles
**Date:** 2026-04-16
**Goal:** Preventative upgrade of clutch master cylinder (CMC) and clutch slave cylinder (CSC)

---

## 1. Why the 8th Gen SI CMC/CSC Fails

### Common Failure Modes

The 8th gen Civic SI (2006-2011, FA5 sedan / FG2 coupe) is notorious in the Honda community for clutch hydraulic failures. The two primary failure modes are:

1. **Soft / spongy clutch pedal** — The pedal gradually loses firmness. Engagement point drifts toward the floor. Eventually the clutch won't fully disengage, making shifting difficult or impossible.
2. **Internal seal bypass (no visible external leak)** — The most frustrating failure mode. Fluid bypasses the internal seals inside the CMC under pressure, so the pedal sinks to the floor under use (especially in stop-and-go traffic or aggressive driving) but recovers after sitting. The reservoir stays full, there's no puddle on the ground, and it looks fine from the outside.
3. **External leaking** — Less common initially but happens as seals deteriorate further. Fluid weeps from the CMC pushrod seal (visible on the firewall inside the cabin near the pedal) or from the CSC at the transmission bellhousing.

### Typical Failure Mileage

- **60,000–100,000 miles** is the most commonly reported window for the first CMC failure on 8th gen SIs.
- Some owners report issues as early as 40,000–50,000 miles, particularly in hot climates or with aggressive driving.
- By 120,000–150,000 miles, the vast majority of 8th gen SIs have had at least one CMC replacement if still on the original unit.
- The CSC tends to last somewhat longer than the CMC but often fails within 20,000–40,000 miles of the CMC going.
- At 170,000 miles, if the originals are still in there, they are on extremely borrowed time (or have already been replaced at some point — worth checking service records).

### Root Causes

1. **Inferior internal seal material** — The 8th gen SI CMC uses rubber cup seals that swell, harden, and crack over time. Honda used a lower-grade rubber compound in the 8th gen SI compared to what was used in the RSX, S2000, and even the base model Civic.
2. **Heat soak** — The CMC is mounted on the firewall directly behind the engine. The K20Z3 runs hot, and the CMC gets significant heat soak — especially in traffic. Heat accelerates seal degradation.
3. **Fluid contamination / moisture absorption** — DOT 3/DOT 4 brake fluid is hygroscopic (absorbs moisture from the air). The 8th gen clutch fluid is rarely flushed during routine maintenance. Moisture-contaminated fluid lowers the boiling point and attacks rubber seals from the inside.
4. **Bore surface finish** — The stock CMC has an aluminum bore with a relatively basic surface finish. As the seals degrade, they can score the bore, which further accelerates leakage past the seals.
5. **Small bore diameter** — The 8th gen SI CMC bore is small (roughly 5/8" or 15.87mm), which means it operates at higher hydraulic pressures for a given pedal force. This puts more stress on the seals.

### CMC vs CSC — How to Tell Which One Is Failing

| Symptom | CMC (Master) | CSC (Slave) |
|---|---|---|
| Pedal slowly sinks to floor when held | Yes (classic sign) | No |
| Pedal is soft/spongy but stays in position | Could be either (air in system) | Could be either |
| Fluid on driver's side carpet or firewall | Yes — leaking past pushrod seal | No |
| Fluid weeping near transmission bellhousing | No | Yes |
| Reservoir level dropping with no visible leak | CMC internal bypass (most common) | Possible CSC internal leak |
| Clutch won't disengage after sitting in traffic | Yes — heat soak causing seal bypass | Less likely |
| Pedal feels fine cold, gets soft when hot | Yes — classic heat-related CMC failure | Less likely |
| Hard pedal / pedal won't return | Rare CMC issue (sticking piston) | More commonly a CSC issue or throwout bearing |

**The "parking lot test":** Pump the clutch pedal 15-20 times rapidly. If the engagement point drops noticeably and the pedal feels mushier, the CMC is bypassing internally.

### TSBs and Recalls

- **No official recall** was issued for the CMC/CSC on the 8th gen SI.
- **TSB 07-006** (early 2007) addressed clutch judder and chatter on 2006-2007 SI models but was related to the clutch disc, not hydraulics.
- Honda did quietly revise the CMC part number during the 8th gen production run (see part numbers below), suggesting they were aware of the issue, but never issued a formal TSB specifically for CMC/CSC failure.
- The Honda community widely regards this as a "known but unacknowledged" design weakness.

## 2. Stock CMC/CSC Specifications

### OEM Part Numbers

**Clutch Master Cylinder (CMC):**
- **46920-SVA-A01** — Original part number (2006-2008 models, early production)
- **46920-SVA-A02** — Revised part number (later production and replacement)
- **46920-SVA-A03** — Further revised (current replacement part from Honda)
- Approximate dealer price: $80–$120

**Clutch Slave Cylinder (CSC):**
- **46930-SVA-A01** — Original
- **46930-SVA-A02** — Revised
- Approximate dealer price: $50–$80

**Note:** The revisions are primarily to the internal seals. The physical dimensions and mounting remained the same across all revisions.

### Stock CMC Specifications
- **Bore diameter:** 5/8" (15.87mm)
- **Body material:** Aluminum
- **Seals:** Rubber cup seals (EPDM-type)
- **Reservoir:** Integrated plastic reservoir with cap
- **Mounting:** Two-bolt flange on firewall, pushrod connects to clutch pedal
- **Fluid type specified:** DOT 3

### Stock CSC Specifications
- **Bore diameter:** Approximately 1" (25.4mm) — larger than the CMC to provide mechanical advantage
- **Body material:** Aluminum
- **Seals:** Rubber cup seals
- **Mounting:** Bolts to the transmission bellhousing, actuates the clutch fork
- **Connection:** Hard line from CMC to a flex hose, then to the CSC

### Why the Stock Units Are Inferior

Compared to the RSX (DC5), S2000 (AP1/AP2), and even the 7th gen Accord V6 6MT:

1. **Cheaper seal compounds** — Cost-cutting during the 8th gen SI's development cycle. The SI was Honda's entry-level sport compact; the RSX Type S and S2000 were premium products with better component sourcing.
2. **No seal revision from Honda until failures were widespread** — The initial design went to production without adequate thermal testing for the seal life.
3. **Less robust bore finish** — The RSX CMC has a better honed bore surface that causes less seal wear.
4. **Packaging / heat exposure** — The 8th gen engine bay is tighter than the RSX (EP3/DC5 platform). More heat reaches the CMC.

## 3. The RSX / DC5 Upgrade Path

### What the Community Means by "RSX CMC/CSC Swap"

This is the most popular and well-proven upgrade for the 8th gen SI clutch hydraulics. The core idea: the 2002-2006 Acura RSX Type S (DC5) used a physically larger, better-built CMC and CSC that can be adapted to fit the 8th gen SI with minimal modification.

The RSX clutch hydraulics were over-engineered relative to the platform — they share lineage with the Integra Type R (DC2) hydraulics and were designed to handle the K20A2's higher-revving, more aggressive use case. Honda used better materials and seals in the RSX units.

### Specific RSX Parts Used

**RSX Type S CMC (2002-2006):**
- **46920-S6M-A03** (or -A01, -A02 for earlier revisions)
- Bore size: 5/8" (same as 8th gen SI — direct hydraulic ratio compatibility)
- Better internal seals (Viton-type or higher-grade EPDM)
- More robust bore honing
- **This is the key upgrade piece**

**RSX Type S CSC (2002-2006):**
- **46930-S6M-A03** (or -A01, -A02)
- Better seals, same general design philosophy
- Direct bolt-on to the 8th gen SI transmission (same bellhousing bolt pattern — both use K-series transmissions)

**Important:** Use the **Type S** parts, not the RSX base model. The base RSX had a different transmission and potentially different CSC mounting. The Type S K20A2 transmission is in the same family as the K20Z3 transmission in the 8th gen SI.

### Why RSX Units Are Better

1. **Better seal material** — Higher-grade rubber or Viton compounds that resist heat and fluid degradation far longer.
2. **Better bore finish** — Smoother honing means less seal wear and better sealing over time.
3. **Proven longevity** — RSX Type S owners routinely report 150,000–200,000+ miles on original hydraulics with basic fluid maintenance. The failure rate is dramatically lower than the 8th gen SI.
4. **Same hydraulic ratio** — Because the bore sizes are the same (5/8" CMC, ~1" CSC), the pedal feel and clutch engagement characteristics remain essentially stock. No change to pedal effort or travel.

### Fitment — Does It Bolt Directly In?

**CMC (Master):**
- The RSX CMC **does not directly bolt to the 8th gen SI firewall** without modification. The mounting bolt pattern and pushrod geometry are different.
- **Options:**
  - **Adapter bracket** — Companies like Hybrid Racing sell adapter brackets that allow the RSX CMC to bolt to the 8th gen SI firewall. This is the clean, reversible solution.
  - **Drill new holes** — Some DIYers drill new mounting holes in the firewall. Works but is permanent and less elegant.
  - **Hybrid Racing complete kit** — Includes the bracket (see Section 4).

**CSC (Slave):**
- The RSX Type S CSC **is a direct bolt-on** to the 8th gen SI transmission. Same bolt pattern, same clutch fork interface. No adapter needed.

**Clutch Line:**
- The stock 8th gen SI hard line / flex hose routing is different from the RSX. When swapping both CMC and CSC, you typically need a **new clutch line** that connects the RSX CMC output fitting to the 8th gen CSC/hard line inlet. Hybrid Racing and other companies sell these.
- Alternatively, many owners take this opportunity to run a **stainless steel braided clutch line** from CMC to CSC (replacing the entire OEM hard line + flex hose assembly). This is the recommended approach.

### What Else Is Needed for the Swap

1. RSX Type S CMC (or Hybrid Racing kit CMC)
2. RSX Type S CSC (or Hybrid Racing kit CSC)
3. Clutch line (stainless braided recommended)
4. Adapter bracket for CMC (if using RSX CMC on 8th gen firewall)
5. Fresh DOT 4 brake fluid (1 quart minimum)
6. Bleeder kit (vacuum bleeder or two-person manual bleeding setup)
7. New crush washers / banjo bolt washers (usually included with aftermarket lines)
8. Shop towels and fluid catch — brake fluid destroys paint

### Clutch Fluid Notes

- **DOT 3** is what Honda specifies from the factory. It works but has a lower boiling point and degrades faster.
- **DOT 4** is the community-recommended upgrade. Higher boiling point, better heat resistance. Fully compatible with Honda clutch hydraulics.
- **Recommended brands:**
  - **Motul RBF 600** — Dry boiling point 594F / Wet 421F. The enthusiast favorite. Amber color makes it easy to see when it's dirty.
  - **ATE TYP 200 (Gold)** — Dry 536F / Wet 396F. Great alternative at a lower price. Use the gold-colored version and alternate with the blue version at each flush so you can visually confirm old fluid is fully purged.
  - **Honda Genuine DOT 4** — Perfectly fine if you want OEM. Cheaper than Motul/ATE.
- **Do NOT use DOT 5 (silicone-based).** DOT 5 is not compatible with Honda hydraulic systems. It does not mix with DOT 3/4 and will destroy seals.
- **DOT 5.1** is glycol-based (like DOT 3/4) and IS compatible, but offers no meaningful advantage over good DOT 4 for this application.

### Bleeder Tools

- **Best option:** Motive Products Power Bleeder (pressure bleeder) — hooks to the CMC reservoir and pressurizes the system. One-person operation. ~$50–$70.
- **Good option:** Mityvac hand vacuum pump — attaches to the bleeder valve on the CSC and pulls fluid through. ~$30–$40.
- **Free option:** Two-person manual bleeding. One person pumps the pedal, the other opens/closes the bleeder valve. Works fine, just slower.
- **Speed Bleeder valves** (one-way check valves that replace the stock bleeder screws) — allow one-person manual bleeding. ~$10–$15 each. Highly recommended for future fluid changes.

## 7. Installation Overview

### Difficulty Level

**Moderate DIY** — 6/10 difficulty.

If you can do brake pads and rotors, you can do this. The hardest part is bleeding the system properly. The actual physical swap is straightforward. Having done any clutch or brake hydraulic work before is a big advantage.

### Time Estimate

- **First-timer:** 3–5 hours (including bleeding, which takes the most time)
- **Experienced:** 1.5–2.5 hours
- **Shop labor:** Typically quoted at 2–3 hours

### Key Steps

**Preparation:**
1. Gather all parts and tools. Have extra fluid on hand — you'll use more than you think during bleeding.
2. Cover the fender, engine bay, and any painted surfaces near the master cylinder with towels or plastic. Brake fluid destroys paint on contact.
3. Have a drip pan ready.

**CMC Removal (Engine Bay Side):**
1. Disconnect the clutch line from the CMC (banjo bolt or flare fitting — depends on your line type). Have a rag ready; fluid will drip.
2. Siphon or drain the old fluid from the CMC reservoir.
3. From inside the cabin, disconnect the CMC pushrod from the clutch pedal. There is a clevis pin with a cotter pin or clip — remove the clip and slide the pin out.
4. Remove the two mounting bolts holding the CMC to the firewall (engine bay side).
5. Pull the CMC out through the engine bay.

**Does the Dash Need to Come Apart?**
- **No.** You do NOT need to remove the dashboard. The CMC pushrod-to-pedal connection is accessible under the dash on the driver's side by simply lying on your back and looking up at the pedal assembly. It's tight but manageable.
- You may want to remove the lower driver's side kick panel (a few clips) for better access to the clevis pin. This takes 2 minutes.

**CMC Installation:**
1. If using Hybrid Racing kit: bolt the adapter bracket to the new CMC per their instructions.
2. Bench bleed the new CMC before installing (see below).
3. Insert the new CMC through the firewall. Attach the pushrod to the clutch pedal (clevis pin + new cotter pin).
4. Bolt the CMC to the firewall.
5. Connect the clutch line to the CMC.

**CSC Removal:**
1. The CSC is bolted to the outside of the transmission bellhousing, accessible from under the car.
2. Raise the car on jack stands or a lift.
3. Disconnect the clutch line from the CSC.
4. Remove the two bolts holding the CSC to the bellhousing.
5. Pull the CSC out. Some fluid will drip.

**CSC Installation:**
1. Install the new CSC with the two mounting bolts. Torque to spec (~16 ft-lbs).
2. Connect the clutch line to the CSC.

**Clutch Line Replacement (if upgrading to SS braided):**
1. The OEM line runs from the CMC (engine bay, driver's side firewall) down along the firewall, through a hard line section, then a flex hose section to the CSC on the transmission.
2. If replacing with a single SS braided line, remove the entire OEM line (disconnect at CMC, disconnect at CSC, remove any clips/brackets holding the hard line to the chassis).
3. Route the new SS braided line from CMC to CSC. Use zip ties or P-clamps to secure it to the chassis so it doesn't hang or contact the exhaust.

### Bench Bleeding the CMC

**This step is critical.** Skipping it will make bleeding the installed system 10x harder.

1. Clamp the new CMC in a vise (lightly, use soft jaws).
2. Fill the reservoir with fresh DOT 4 fluid.
3. Use a bench bleed kit (two short hoses that loop from the output port(s) back into the reservoir) — or fabricate one with clear tubing.
4. Slowly push the CMC piston in and let it return. You'll see air bubbles come out through the tube into the reservoir.
5. Repeat 20–30 times until no more bubbles appear.
6. Cap or plug the output port to prevent air from re-entering.
7. Install the CMC immediately.

### Bleeding the Installed System

**Proper Bleeding Sequence:**

1. Fill the CMC reservoir to the MAX line with fresh DOT 4.
2. Start at the CSC bleeder valve (the furthest point from the CMC).
3. **If using a pressure bleeder:** Attach to CMC reservoir, pressurize to 10–15 PSI, open CSC bleeder, let fluid flow until clear with no bubbles, close bleeder.
4. **If using a vacuum bleeder:** Attach to CSC bleeder, open bleeder, pump vacuum to pull fluid through. Keep the CMC reservoir topped off.
5. **If manual bleeding:** Have a helper pump the clutch pedal 3–4 times and hold it down. Open the bleeder valve to release fluid/air. Close the bleeder. Helper releases pedal. Repeat until fluid comes out clean with no bubbles.
6. After bleeding, pump the clutch pedal 20–30 times. The pedal should be firm with a consistent engagement point.
7. If the pedal is still soft, repeat the bleeding process. Air trapped in the system is the #1 cause of post-install problems.
8. Check for leaks at every connection point.
9. Top off the reservoir to the MAX line.

### Gotchas and Tips

- **Do not let the reservoir run dry during bleeding.** If air enters the CMC, you have to start over (or nearly).
- **Use a turkey baster to siphon old fluid from the reservoir** before starting work. This minimizes the mess.
- **Brake fluid on paint = instant damage.** Keep a spray bottle of water handy to immediately rinse any spills.
- **New crush washers every time** you disconnect a banjo bolt. Reusing old crush washers is the #1 cause of slow seeping leaks.
- **Torque the banjo bolts properly.** Over-torquing cracks the fitting; under-torquing leaks. Typically 25 ft-lbs for Honda banjo bolts.
- **The clevis pin under the dash is fiddly.** A small flashlight and a magnetic pickup tool make it much easier. Some people remove the driver's seat for more room, but it's not strictly necessary.
- **If the pedal feel is different after the swap,** allow 50–100 miles for the seals to seat and for any micro-air to work itself out. A second bleed after a week of driving is good practice.

## 8. Maintenance Notes

### Clutch Fluid Flush Interval

- **Honda's recommendation:** No specific interval in the maintenance schedule (this is part of the problem).
- **Community recommendation:** **Every 2 years or 30,000 miles**, whichever comes first.
- **Track use / aggressive driving:** Every year or 15,000 miles.
- Clutch fluid shares a reservoir with nothing else (it is separate from the brake fluid system on the 8th gen SI), so flushing it is quick and cheap. There is no reason not to do it regularly.

### Signs the CMC/CSC Need Attention

- Pedal feel changes (softer, mushier, longer travel to engagement)
- Engagement point shifts (especially sinking toward the floor)
- Clutch pedal slowly sinks to the floor when held steady
- Difficulty getting into gear, especially 1st and reverse
- Fluid in the reservoir is dark brown or black (should be clear amber if DOT 4)
- Fluid level in the reservoir has dropped without explanation
- Wet spot on the firewall (driver's side, under the dash) or near the transmission bellhousing

### Is This a "Do It Once and Forget It" Upgrade?

**Mostly yes, with basic maintenance:**

- The RSX / Hybrid Racing CMC and CSC are fundamentally better units that last much longer. Owners routinely report 100,000+ miles with no issues after the upgrade.
- **However**, you still need to flush the clutch fluid every 2 years / 30,000 miles. No hydraulic system is "maintenance-free" — the fluid still absorbs moisture and degrades over time.
- If you install the upgraded units and flush the fluid on schedule, you should never have to think about the clutch hydraulics again for the remaining life of the car.
- The stainless steel braided clutch line is essentially a lifetime part — no degradation, no expansion under pressure. It never needs replacement under normal use.

### Maintenance Checklist (Post-Upgrade)

- [ ] Flush clutch fluid every 2 years / 30,000 miles with fresh DOT 4
- [ ] Visually inspect CMC reservoir level at every oil change
- [ ] Check for leaks at CMC, CSC, and all line fittings annually
- [ ] If the pedal ever feels different, bleed the system immediately — don't wait

## Summary

The 8th gen Civic SI (FG2/FA5) has a well-documented weakness in its clutch hydraulics. At 170,000 miles, upgrading to RSX-sourced or Hybrid Racing CMC/CSC units is a smart preventative move. The Hybrid Racing kit is the cleanest, best-supported solution. Paired with a stainless steel braided clutch line and DOT 4 fluid, this is a one-afternoon job that eliminates the most common reliability concern on the 8th gen SI platform.

**Recommended total investment:** $280–$500 in parts, 3–5 hours of DIY time.

**Bottom line:** Do the Hybrid Racing CMC + RSX OEM CSC + SS braided clutch line + Motul RBF 600 DOT 4 flush. Then flush the fluid every 2 years. Never worry about it again.
